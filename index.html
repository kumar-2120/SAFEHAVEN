What Is Domestic Violence?

Domestic violence is a pattern of behaviour used by one person to gain power and control over another in an intimate or family relationship. It can affect anyone regardless of age, gender, race, religion, or social status.

👊

Physical Abuse

Hitting, slapping, kicking, choking, or any unwanted physical contact intended to hurt, injure, or intimidate.

🗣️

Emotional & Verbal Abuse

Constant criticism, humiliation, threats, isolation from friends and family, and manipulation that damages self-worth.

💰

Financial Abuse

Controlling access to money, preventing employment, creating debt, or ruining credit to trap someone in a relationship.

📱

Digital Abuse

Using technology to stalk, harass, or control — including monitoring devices, threatening messages, or sharing private content.

🔒

Coercive Control

A pattern of intimidation and control that strips away freedom — limiting who a person can see, where they can go, and what they can do.

❤️‍🩹

Sexual Abuse

Any unwanted sexual contact or behaviour. Consent can be withdrawn at any time; no relationship status changes that.

Recognising the Warning Signs

Abuse is not always obvious. Some signs are subtle at first. Trust your instincts — if something feels wrong, it probably is.

  • 😰 Feeling afraid of your partner and walking on eggshells
  • 🚪 Being prevented from seeing friends or family
  • 📵 Partner checks your phone, email, or location constantly
  • 💬 Constant put-downs, name-calling, or public humiliation
  • 💸 Having no access to or control over money
  • 😶 Partner makes all decisions for you without input
  • 🤕 Unexplained injuries or excuses for bruises/marks
  • 😔 Feeling like you deserve the treatment or it's your fault
  • 🧍 Partner threatens harm to you, your children, or pets
  • 🏠 Unable to leave the house without permission
  • 😟 Feeling worthless, hopeless, or deeply anxious at home
  • 📞 Partner listens to phone calls or reads messages

Steps to Safety

Leaving an abusive situation can feel overwhelming. Take it one step at a time — help is available at every stage.

  1. Acknowledge what's happening

    Recognising that what you're experiencing is abuse is a courageous and important first step.

  2. Reach out to a trusted person

    A friend, family member, colleague, or a professional counsellor — you don't have to carry this alone.

  3. Call a hotline

    Hotline advocates can help you understand your options, make a safety plan, and locate local resources.

  4. Create a safety plan

    Identify a safe place to go, gather important documents, and plan your exit in advance if possible.

  5. Seek legal protection

    Consider a protective order or restraining order. Many courts and legal aid organisations offer free assistance.

  6. Access shelter & services

    Local shelters, housing services, and community organisations can provide immediate safety and long-term support.

  7. Healing & recovery

    Therapy, support groups, and counselling can help survivors rebuild confidence, safety, and independence.
